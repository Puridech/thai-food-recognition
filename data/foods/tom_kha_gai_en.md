## üåü Cultural Story and History

Tom Kha Gai is one of the most beloved Thai soups both domestically and internationally, originating from Central Thailand. It's believed to have evolved from the traditional Thai lifestyle that heavily features coconut milk and aromatic herbs in cooking. The name "Tom Kha" comes from the cooking method "Tom" (to boil) and "Kha" (galangal), the signature herb that gives this soup its distinctive fragrant aroma.

What makes Tom Kha Gai special is its perfectly balanced combination of flavors: the richness of coconut milk, the refreshing sourness of lime juice, the aromatic fragrance of galangal and lemongrass, and the subtle heat from fish sauce and chilies. This creates a complex yet harmonious soup that differs from Tom Yum, which has a more intense spicy and sour profile. Tom Kha Gai offers a milder, creamier taste that appeals to those who prefer less fiery dishes.

This soup can be enjoyed year-round, but it's especially popular during the rainy season when the weather is cool and refreshing, or when someone feels under the weather. Galangal has traditional medicinal properties believed to help with digestion and relieve coughs, while coconut milk provides warmth and comfort. It's also a favorite at social gatherings, Thai restaurants both in Thailand and abroad, and has become one of the most recognized and loved Thai dishes by international food enthusiasts.

Today, Tom Kha Gai has become an iconic representation of Thai cuisine worldwide. It has been adapted into various versions such as Tom Kha Goong (with shrimp), Tom Kha Talay (with mixed seafood), Tom Kha Pla (with fish), or even vegetarian versions with tofu and mushrooms. However, the original recipe using chicken remains the most popular and widely accepted version, offering a perfectly balanced and comforting flavor profile.

### Ingredients (Serves 2-3)

#### Main Ingredients
- 300g chicken breast or thigh, cut into bite-sized pieces
- 2 cups coconut milk (or 400ml canned coconut milk)
- 1 cup chicken stock or water

#### Aromatics
- 50g galangal, thinly sliced (about 8-10 slices)
- 2 lemongrass stalks, cut into 1.5-inch pieces and bruised
- 5-6 kaffir lime leaves, torn in half
- 2 shallots, sliced (optional)

#### Seasonings
- 2-3 tablespoons fish sauce
- 2-3 tablespoons fresh lime juice
- 1 teaspoon sugar
- 5-7 bird's eye chilies, lightly crushed (adjust to taste)
- 1 tablespoon vegetable oil

#### Garnishes/Vegetables
- 150g straw mushrooms or button mushrooms, halved
- 4 cherry tomatoes, quartered
- Fresh cilantro for garnish
- Fresh red chilies, sliced (for garnish)

---

### Cooking Instructions

**Step 1: Prepare Ingredients**  
Wash the chicken thoroughly and cut into bite-sized pieces. Clean and halve the mushrooms. Quarter the cherry tomatoes. Prepare thinly sliced galangal, cut lemongrass into 1.5-inch pieces and bruise them, and tear kaffir lime leaves in half.

**Step 2: Heat the Coconut Milk**  
Place a pot over medium heat. Add 1 cup of coconut milk and bring to a gentle simmer. Add the galangal, lemongrass, and kaffir lime leaves. Simmer for 3-4 minutes to allow the aromatic herbs to release their fragrance into the coconut milk.

**Step 3: Add Chicken and Mushrooms**  
Add the chicken pieces to the pot. Stir gently and cook until the chicken is cooked through (approximately 3-4 minutes). Then add the straw mushrooms and cherry tomatoes. Continue cooking for another 2-3 minutes until the mushrooms and tomatoes are tender.

**Step 4: Add Remaining Coconut Milk and Stock**  
Pour in the remaining coconut milk and chicken stock (or water). Stir to combine. Bring the soup back to a gentle simmer, then reduce the heat to low. Avoid boiling the soup vigorously as it may cause the coconut milk to separate.

**Step 5: Season the Soup**  
Season with fish sauce, sugar, and lightly crushed bird's eye chilies. Taste and adjust the seasoning to achieve a balanced flavor (should have a good balance of salty, sweet, and a hint of heat). Don't add lime juice yet, as adding it too early will make the soup overly sour.

**Step 6: Turn Off Heat and Add Lime Juice**  
Once you've achieved your desired taste, turn off the heat. Now squeeze in the fresh lime juice and stir gently to combine. Adding lime juice after turning off the heat preserves its fresh, aromatic quality and prevents the soup from becoming too sour.

**Step 7: Serve**  
Ladle the soup into bowls. Garnish with fresh cilantro and sliced red chilies. Serve hot with steamed jasmine rice or sticky rice.

### üí° Tips and Notes

**Tips for Better Taste:**
- Fresh squeezed coconut milk provides better aroma and flavor than canned coconut milk, but if using canned, choose a high-quality brand
- Slice galangal thinly and bruise it lightly to release more aroma
- Don't let the coconut milk boil vigorously; use medium to low heat to prevent separation
- Add lime juice after turning off the heat to preserve its fresh aroma and prevent excessive sourness
- For a richer flavor, you can use coconut cream instead of regular coconut milk

**Important Warnings:**
- Don't overcook the chicken or it will become tough and dry‚Äîcook just until done
- Lightly crush the chilies but don't chop or grind them, as this will make the soup too spicy
- Galangal, lemongrass, and kaffir lime leaves should not be eaten‚Äîremove them before eating or warn diners
- Avoid separating the coconut milk by using excessively high heat; use medium heat and stir frequently
- If the soup becomes too thick, you can thin it with a bit of water or stock

**Serving Suggestions and Pairings:**
- Serve hot with steamed jasmine rice (Tom Kha Gai pairs better with steamed rice than sticky rice)
- Enjoy alongside other main dishes such as stir-fried vegetables, fried fish, or Thai omelet
- Perfect for lunch or dinner, especially on cool, comfortable days
- Can be stored in the refrigerator for 2-3 days; reheat gently without letting the coconut milk boil
- For extra freshness, add cilantro and fresh basil leaves when serving
- This soup is excellent for those new to Thai cuisine, as it's less spicy than Tom Yum but equally flavorful

**Recipe Variations:**
- Substitute chicken with shrimp or fish (Tom Kha Goong/Tom Kha Pla)
- Add variety of mushrooms such as shiitake, oyster mushrooms, or enoki
- For vegetarians, use tofu and mushrooms instead of chicken, and soy sauce instead of fish sauce
- Adjust the spiciness level by adding more or fewer chilies according to preference
- Some recipes include straw mushrooms only, while others mix various types of mushrooms for more texture

**Cultural Notes:**
- Tom Kha Gai is considered comfort food in Thailand and is often served to people who are feeling unwell
- The soup's mild nature makes it accessible to international palates while still offering authentic Thai flavors
- It's commonly served in Thai restaurants worldwide and has become one of the "gateway" dishes introducing people to Thai cuisine
- The balance of hot (from chilies), sour (from lime), salty (from fish sauce), and sweet (from sugar) represents the fundamental principle of Thai cooking
